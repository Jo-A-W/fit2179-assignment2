Chapter I

The Big Picture

Australia is one of the world's largest food exporters, sending over $66.7 billion worth of food products overseas in 2025. Over the past two decades, the value of Australia's food exports has more than doubled — driven by growing demand from Asia and rising global food prices. The COVID-19 pandemic caused only a minor dip in 2020 before a record-breaking surge.

"Australia exports enough food to feed approximately 75 million people annually — nearly three times its own population."

Key Insight

Meat & Livestock has been Australia's largest food export category every year since 2006. By 2025, it accounted for over 42% of total food exports — nearly $28.5 billion. Grains & Cereals surged dramatically in 2021–23 due to global supply shortages following the Russia-Ukraine war.

Chapter II

What Australia Exports

From beef to wheat, Australia exports a remarkably diverse range of food products. Beef consistently ranks as the most valuable single export commodity, followed by wheat and other grains. The product mix has shifted over time — canola and tree nuts are fast-growing categories, while wine exports peaked in the mid-2000s.

Australia's wheat exports tripled between 2020 and 2023, reaching a record $16.7 billion in 2022–23. This was driven by strong global demand following supply disruptions in Ukraine and Russia, which together normally account for around 30% of global wheat exports.

Chapter III

State by State

Australia's agricultural output is spread across the continent, with each state specialising in different commodities based on climate and geography. New South Wales leads in overall output, Western Australia dominates grain production, Queensland leads in beef, and Victoria is the dairy heartland. Together, these four states generate over 90% of Australia's agricultural value.

Chapter IV

How Trade Has Shifted Over Time

Australia's trading relationships have shifted significantly over the past two decades. China has grown to become Australia's dominant food export market, while wheat exports have surged in recent years. Not all trading relationships are balanced — some countries sell Australia more food than they buy from it, resulting in a net food trade deficit with partners like New Zealand and Singapore.

"While China and Japan are Australia's biggest food export markets, New Zealand and Singapore are actually net food import partners — Australia buys more food from them than it sells."
Interactive — hover over bars to compare export & import values

Diverging bar chart: bars extending right = Australian food exports to that country; bars extending left = Australian food imports from that country. Countries where the left bar dominates (New Zealand, Singapore, Thailand) are ones where Australia runs a food trade deficit.

Key Insight

Australia imports significantly more food from New Zealand ($2.1B) than it exports ($850M) — a deficit of over $1.2 billion. This is largely due to dairy products, processed foods, and wine from New Zealand crossing the Tasman. Singapore similarly sells more food to Australia ($963M) than it buys ($650M), primarily through processed and packaged goods.
